# Threat Model – AIC / AIN

## 1. Threat Philosophy

Threat modeling includes:

- External attacks
- Internal corruption
- Ethical subversion
- Slow structural decay

AIC treats ethical erosion as a security threat.

---

## 2. Adversary Categories

### 2.1 External Technical Adversaries

- Code injection
- Registry tampering
- Archive poisoning
- Simulation manipulation

Defense:
- Signed commits
- Immutable content addressing
- Multi-node verification

---

### 2.2 Internal Governance Adversaries

- Privileged policy override
- Hidden amendment
- Authority concentration

Defense:
- Explicit authority layers
- Supermajority requirements
- Transparent audit trails

---

### 2.3 Economic Adversaries

- Profit-driven weakening of safeguards
- Tokenization pressure
- Growth-above-stability incentives

Defense:
- No economic token core
- Built-in ethical immutability
- Constitutional compatibility enforcement

---

### 2.4 Cognitive Adversaries

- Narrative manipulation
- Human trust erosion
- Decision fatigue exploitation

Defense:
- Human delay policies
- Escalation controls
- Transparent rationale logging

---

## 3. Systemic Threat: Accumulation Drift

The greatest threat is not hacking.
It is slow drift toward:

- Centralization
- Speed over responsibility
- Convenience over accountability

AIC is designed to resist drift structurally, not rhetorically.

---

## 4. Zero-Trust Assumption

- No node is fully trusted
- No human is infallible
- No policy is perfect
- No version is final

Trust must be earned and decays over time.
